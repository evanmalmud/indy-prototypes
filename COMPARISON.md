# COMPARISON.md — which pillar actually delivers the aha?

**Stance:** skeptical creative director. Each `NOTES.md` was written by the author
of the prototype it describes, so each is a self-assessment. `AUDIT.md` was
generated by exhaustive search over the pure sims and knows nothing about intent.
**Where the memo and the audit disagree, the audit wins.** A pillar whose levels
mostly fail the forced-interaction test did not deliver ahas, however good the
design memo sounds.

Two things to say up front, because they colour everything below.

**Pillar B is not finished, and it is being graded as if it were.** It has no
`NOTES.md` — the other two do. Its capstone level `b6` has no authored route
(`src/pillar-b/routes.ts` documents the gap honestly) and no win assertion; its
test file is `scratch.test.ts`, 82 lines of `console.log`, not the 438-line
replay suite Pillar A ships. Its page is a 4-line placeholder
(`src/pillar-b/main.ts`), so the only pillar that can kill you is the only one
with no undo. And six comments across `src/pillar-b/levels.ts` and `sim.ts` cite
a `sim.test.ts` that **does not exist** — including `levels.ts:304`, which claims
b6's one-turn window is "a fact about the simulation rather than a claim in a
comment." It is a claim in a comment. Some of B's audit failures are therefore
build-incompleteness rather than pillar-incompleteness, and I flag it where it
matters instead of scoring B for a crime it may not have committed.

**Pillar C is graded on an easier exam than A, and its headline number reflects
that.** `AUDIT.md:39-46` says so itself: for A and B, "forced" means *deleting
the interaction makes the room unsolvable*. For C it means *deleting the
interaction makes the room unable to reach par* — and `par` is a hand-authored
integer with no formula behind it (`src/pillar-c/sim.ts:165`). C's best-in-repo
3.33 forced-interactions-per-level is measured against a bar C's own designer
chose. The correlation is visible in the data: the par-to-best gaps are c1 2,
c2 6, c3 4, c4 8, c5 3, c6 8 — and the single room that failed, c4, is one of the
two with the widest gap. Set par tighter, force more interactions. That is a
dial, not a discovery.

## 1. What each pillar actually feels like to play, as built

### Pillar A — "Clever, not strong"

You walk into a still room and nothing happens, and nothing will ever happen, and
that is the whole contract. Rooms 1 through 3 are competent tutorials that hand
you one verb each and then ask you to use it once; a1 is 17 moves for two
interactions and a2 is 11 for two, and neither will be remembered. Then a4
happens, and a4 is the best fifteen minutes in this repository. You are given a
boulder you cannot push because the tile you would push from is wall and the
water denies you the whole east half of the room, and — critically — a *second*
boulder in the middle of the floor that you *can* push, freely, in every
direction, so you spend real turns wedging it into a corner and resetting before
it occurs to you that push might not be the verb. The audit proves you are not
imagining the wall: 20 moves is optimal, the room's own test exhausts all 781
push-only states and finds no win, and the win appears the instant the whip is
enabled. Then a5 and a6 arrive and you notice you are being asked to *arrange* a
verb you already own rather than find a new one — A's own memo admits it ("levels
5 and 6 arrange a verb the player already owns, and I felt the drop while
authoring them", `NOTES.md:12-14`). Play as built: three warm-ups, one genuinely
great room, two competent recombinations, and a stuck player whose only failure
mode is closing the tab.

### Pillar B — "The tomb is alive"

Mostly you walk east. That is not a joke about pacing, it is the audit's actual
finding: b2's shortest win is `E E E E E E E E E E TE E E` — ten steps along the
top corridor, torch the vine, two more steps — a route that never once touches the
oil trail the room is named after and exists to teach. b6, the capstone, is won in
eleven moves by `S S E E E SE W W W S S`, firing exactly one interaction out of
the five it declares. In between, b3 and b4 are real and good: b3 forces 3 of 3
with `COLLAPSE+BOULDER` genuinely load-bearing, b4 forces 2 of 2, and the beat of
pulling a boulder into a flooding channel is the best thing in the pillar. But the
median experience is a room advertising a five-system chain that you solve by
walking past it, because six escalation systems ticking in a documented order
produce a board so busy that the shortest path through it is usually the one that
ignores everything. B's own sim header says the quiet part: "six systems ticking in
an undocumented order is a random number generator wearing a trench coat." The
telegraph layer built to make this legible (`sim.ts:1201`) has no renderer. As
built, B is 1,306 lines of simulation that the player cannot see and mostly does
not need.

### Pillar C — "Greed is the real enemy"

The door is never locked. You can walk out of every room in this pillar in a
handful of moves with empty hands and a score of zero — c5's proven escape is
literally three steps south from spawn. So the *puzzle* is gone; the average
shortest route in C is 8.7 moves against A's 18.2, and no room in C is hard. What
replaces it is a shopping problem, and it is a genuinely good one. c3 is the same
room at 54, 32, or -5 depending on which of three prices you pay; c6 spans 73
against 48 on the single question of which pool pays the door. On a second run
this is excellent — every purchase is a real comparison against a route you now
know. On the first run it is tense in a bad way, because you are spending without
knowing what is around the corner, which is guessing rather than deciding, and C's
memo says exactly this (`NOTES.md:33-37`). The thing that should worry Evan most
is what pricing does to curiosity: every tool use is a small irreversible payment,
so the rational answer to "I wonder what the whip does to that boulder" is *don't
find out*. C's memo is admirably blunt about it — "a resource you can lose is a
resource you stop experimenting with" — and then names the flagship as its own
biggest risk, because `WHIP+BOULDER` costs 8 points and a player who has just
realised they can *pull* should be exhilarated, not doing arithmetic.

## 3. Where each pillar is lying to itself

**Pillar A** tells itself that a4 generalises. The memo's framing is that the pull
"retroactively re-opens every room you already walked through" — and that is the
most seductive sentence in this repo, because it promises content multiplication
for free. Nothing in the audit supports it. a1 is 17 moves forcing 2 interactions
in a 100-state space; walking back in with the whip does not give you a new
puzzle, it gives you a *shortcut*, because a1 has no second objective the pull
could unlock. Re-opening a room only creates a new puzzle if the room contains
something you previously could not reach — and Pillar A has no collectibles, no
optional objectives, and nothing in any room except the exit. The retroactive
promise is real *inside* a4, where the decoy boulder makes you exhaust pushing
before the reveal lands, and it is currently vapour everywhere else. A's honest
position is that it has proved one room, not a pillar.

**Pillar B** tells itself that its rooms are precision instruments. b6's design
comment is the longest in the pillar — 45 lines describing a four-system chain
with exactly one correct turn to strike the match — and ends with "the room is
solvable because of the documented resolution order and for no other reason,
which is either the best or the worst thing about this pillar." The audit
answers: it is the worst. b6 is won in eleven moves by `S S E E E SE W W W S S`,
which fires one interaction and none of the five declared, and the room has no
authored route proving the intended line is even reachable. The seductive claim
is that escalation creates urgency; the measurement is that escalation creates
*busyness*, and a busy board's shortest path is usually the one that outruns
every system instead of engaging one. B's forced-per-level of 1.17 is the lowest
in the repo, and it is lowest in the pillar whose entire premise is that the room
applies pressure. Pressure that can be walked past is set dressing.

**Pillar C** tells itself that 3.33 forced interactions per level makes it the
winner. It is the number C would put on a slide, and it is measured against a
different denominator than A's 2.00 — "still reaches par" instead of "still
solvable" — with par hand-authored per level and no formula anywhere
(`sim.ts:165`). The audit flags this itself in caveat 2. Worse, the caveat below
it (AUDIT.md:43) undercuts the pillar more deeply than C's memo admits: C **cannot
be lost and does not require carrying treasure out**, so the cheapest route is
nearly always the empty-handed walk, and the average shortest route across all
six rooms is 8.7 moves. C has not built hard rooms with interesting prices; it has
built easy rooms with interesting prices, and it is scoring itself on the prices.
The deeper self-deception is the one C's own memo *almost* reaches and then steps
back from: it says scarcity suppresses experimentation and calls this a "risk."
It is not a risk, it is a measured property of the design. Evan's stated goal is
players discovering how tools interact. A pillar whose central mechanic makes
players stop poking things is not a variant of that goal, it is opposed to it.

## 4. THE INTERACTION QUESTION

**Which interactions produced the best levels, which fell flat, and is the aha
coming from the interactions or from the pillar wrapped around them?**

Take the whole repo as one experiment. Eighteen rooms, three wrappers, one shared
interaction table. If the *pillar* is what generates aha, then aha strength should
cluster by pillar. If the *interactions* generate it, strength should cluster by
interaction and cut across pillars. The audit rollup answers this cleanly, and it
answers it in favour of the interactions.

**The interactions that worked, and where:**

`WHIP+BOULDER` is the only interaction in this project that appears in all three
pillars — a4, a5, a6 in the static tomb; b3 and b4 in the collapsing one; c1 and
c6 in the priced one — and it is **forced in every single room it appears in, in
every pillar**. Seven rooms, three utterly different wrappers, zero bypasses. That
is the strongest signal in the entire dataset and it is a signal about the *verb*,
not the frame. The pull does not need pressure to be interesting and it does not
need a price; it inverts a rule the player brought with them from every other
puzzle game they have ever played, and that inversion is self-sufficient.

`REVOLVER+ROPE` is the quiet second-best and confirms the pattern: forced in a6,
c3, and c6 — two pillars, three rooms, no failures. A's memo calls it "the
surprise second-best" and the reason is the same as the pull's: it relocates what
the player thinks they are aiming at. Again, no wrapper required.

`COLLAPSE+BOULDER` and `FLOOD+BOULDER` (b3, b4) are the two genuinely good things
Pillar B produced, and they are notable because they are *boulder* interactions.
Every interaction that has ever worked in this repo across more than one room
moves a heavy object into a place it was not.

**The interactions that fell flat, and where:**

`SATCHEL+PRESSURE_PLATE` is the diagnostic case, and it is the one that decides
this question. It is the *only* interaction to be flagged unforced in two
different pillars: b6 (0/5 declared, AUDIT.md:109) and c4 (AHA NOT FORCED,
AUDIT.md:146). Same interaction, two different wrappers, same failure. A's memo,
written independently and with no access to B's or C's audit rows, calls it "the
flattest" for a design reason — the Raiders reference means players "arrive
already *knowing*, so it plays as a tutorial, not a discovery." **Three
independent lines of evidence, from three separate prototypes, converge on the
same verb.** If the pillar were the variable, this interaction would have failed
in one pillar and worked in the others. It failed everywhere it was load-bearing.

`TORCH+OIL_TRAIL` is the most damning result in the repo and it deserves stating
plainly: **it has never once been forced, anywhere.** It is declared in b2 and in
b6, and the audit shows 0/2 and 0/5. This is the interaction the design brief
calls the one that "turns time into a puzzle element rather than just a resource",
and it is *structurally impossible* in A and C — both memos independently
identify it as needing a turn-count clock, which only B has. So the pillar built
specifically to host it had two attempts and forced it zero times. The bridge
mechanic between the pillars is, as shipped, load-bearing in no room in this
project.

`WHIP+TORCH` (fling / remote ignition) and `REVOLVER+GAS_VENT` are worse still:
they appear in **zero shipped levels in any pillar**. Fling is fully implemented
(`pillar-a/sim.ts:545-564`), the unlit-brazier lock it feeds is implemented
(`sim.ts:608`), and no room in the repository contains a brazier or a torch item.
It is dead code shipping as a designed aha.

So of the six seeded ahas: two are validated and strong across all pillars, one is
measurably flat in two pillars, and **three have never been forced in a single
room in the entire project.** Half the aha set is unvalidated, and that fact is
invariant to which pillar you pick.

### The answer

**The aha is coming from the interactions, not from the pillar.** Three
convergent reasons:

1. **The best interaction is pillar-invariant.** `WHIP+BOULDER` is forced in 7/7
   rooms across all three wrappers. Nothing about static-vs-escalating-vs-priced
   changed how well it worked.
2. **The failures cluster by interaction, not by pillar.** `SATCHEL+PRESSURE_PLATE`
   failed in B *and* in C. If the wrapper drove aha strength, failure would sort by
   wrapper. It sorts by verb.
3. **The pillar that most aggressively wrapped the interactions did worst at
   delivering them.** B has the most machinery — six tick systems, 1,306 lines —
   and the lowest forced-per-level (1.17) and the most unforced ahas (3). The
   pillar that added *nothing* (A: static, no pressure, no economy) has the
   cleanest sheet: 6/6 GOOD, zero unforced ahas.

The honest counter-argument, which I will not hide: B's failures are confounded
with B being unfinished, and C's forced-per-level of 3.33 is the highest number in
the repo. But C's number is measured on a softer bar it set for itself, and the
thing C's forcing actually forces is *choosing among known verbs* — which is
selection pressure, not discovery. C's own memo concedes the split in its first
paragraph and lands on the same side I have: scarcity makes choosing better and
makes discovering worse.

**What this means for Evan's decision.** The pillar is not the product. It is
packaging around a verb set whose quality varies by a factor the packaging does
not move. So the pillar should be chosen on **cost, not on aha**, and the
engineering effort that would have gone into a pillar should go into the
interaction table instead — where three of six designed ahas are currently
unvalidated and 15 of 20 matrix cells are empty. Pick the cheapest wrapper that
does not actively suppress experimentation. That is Pillar A, on every cost metric
in the table above, and it is also the only wrapper that does not charge the player
for curiosity.

## 5. THE HYBRID QUESTION

The pillars are not exclusive. Three concrete hybrids, evaluated honestly.

### Hybrid 1 — **A-spine + ability-gating** (the one Evan is most interested in)

Ship rooms in Pillar A's static frame, but gate tools: the player starts with the
satchel, earns the revolver at room 3, the whip at room 5. Earning a tool re-opens
every earlier room with a new verb. The pitch is N tools × M rooms instead of
N + M, and it directly serves "players discovering how tools interact."

**What is genuinely strong here.** This is not speculative — **a4 already proves
the beat works, inside a single room.** a4's entire pedagogy is ability-gating in
miniature: the decoy boulder exists so the player exhausts pushing, hits the wall,
and *then* the pull lands. `levels.ts:133-140` says so explicitly ("A room where
pushing is *visibly* unavailable teaches nothing; this one has to let them exhaust
it"). Ability-gating is that structure scaled from one room to a campaign, and it
is the only mechanism on the table that makes the pull's retroactive promise real
instead of rhetorical. It also costs almost nothing to build: Pillar A already has
per-level tool loadouts (`LevelDef.tools`, `pillar-a/sim.ts:88`), enforced as the
first check in `doUseTool`. The gating machinery ships today.

**Where the pitch is dishonest, and this is the part to take seriously.**
Re-entering a solved room with a new verb gives you a **faster route, not a new
puzzle** — unless the room contains something you could not previously reach.
Pillar A rooms contain an exit and nothing else. Walk back into a1 with the whip
and you save four moves; you do not get an aha, you get a time trial. The N × M
multiplier is only real if each room has a *gated secondary objective*, and Pillar
A has no such object. **Ability-gating does not work in Pillar A as built.** It
requires importing a collectible — which is Pillar C's treasure layer.

Second honest deduction: even with collectibles, N × M is marketing. A new tool
meaningfully re-opens the two or three earlier rooms whose geometry it happens to
speak to, not all of them. Realistic multiplier is closer to M + 3N than M × N —
still an excellent deal against M + N, but Evan should budget for the smaller
number.

**Verdict: strong, but only as Hybrid 2. On its own it is a shortcut generator.**

### Hybrid 2 — **A-spine + treasure-as-carrot + ability-gating, with the economy deleted**

Take Pillar C's `treasures: Record<string, number>` and `score()` — roughly 20
lines (`pillar-c/sim.ts:244-262`) — and **leave the resource pools behind.**
Treasure becomes an optional objective placed behind a tool the player does not
yet have. Tool uses stay free, so poking everything stays free.

**Why this is the good one.** It separates the two things Pillar C fused. C's
treasure layer is what makes a room have more than one reason to enter it; C's
*scarcity* layer is what makes players stop experimenting. C's memo names the
second as the central risk and never notices the first can be shipped without it.
Hybrid 2 takes the mechanism that gives ability-gating something to gate, and
drops the mechanism that suppresses discovery. It also recovers C's single best
property — replayability, where C scores 5 and A scores 1 — because "I saw an idol
I could not reach" is a reason to come back that costs nothing to author.

**What it costs honestly.** Two systems, neither validated by anything in this
repo. Nothing measures whether a re-opened room lands, and the audit has no metric
for it — every number in `AUDIT.md` grades single rooms in isolation. Building
this means trusting an inference. It also imports part of C's authoring burden:
treasure placement has to be checked against tool availability at each point in
the gate order, which is a search problem, and C's memo is emphatic that this
pillar "is not authorable without that tool." The good news is the tool exists
(`tools/solver.ts`, `tools/audit.ts`) and already handles C.

### Hybrid 3 — **A-spine + exactly one of B's tick systems (fire), imported per-room**

Do not adopt Pillar B. Adopt 76 lines of it. `tickFire` (`pillar-b/sim.ts:768-843`)
is the only escalation system required by a seeded aha — `TORCH+OIL_TRAIL` needs a
turn-count clock and both A's and C's memos independently identify this as why they
cannot host it. Import fire alone, into otherwise-static rooms, for the two or
three rooms that use oil.

**Why this is worth doing.** It buys the one seeded aha that is structurally
impossible in a static tomb for **6% of B's simulation code**, and it does it
without the flood/collapse/guardian/boulder-motion systems that made B's boards
too busy to force anything. B's failure was never fire; b2 and b6 failed because
five systems ran at once and the shortest path outran all of them. One system on
an otherwise-inert board is exactly the condition under which fire is legible.

**What to be careful about.** It breaks A's cleanest property: a still frame stops
being a complete description of the state, so those specific rooms need the
telegraph layer B built and never rendered. Scope it to rooms that contain oil,
and keep the rest of the campaign static — a mixed campaign is fine, a mixed
*renderer* is a real cost. Also note this is the hybrid that could rescue the repo's
worst result: `TORCH+OIL_TRAIL` is currently forced in zero rooms out of two
attempts, and this is the only proposal on the table that gives it a third.

## 6. RECOMMENDATION

**Build Hybrid 2 — Pillar A as the spine, with a treasure layer and ability-gating
and no resource economy — and fold in Hybrid 3's fire tick for the two or three
rooms that need it.**

The argument in one paragraph. Section 4 establishes that the aha lives in the
interactions and not in the wrapper: the flagship pull is forced in 7 of 7 rooms
across all three pillars, and the flat interaction is flat in two different
pillars. If the wrapper does not move aha quality, the wrapper should be chosen on
cost and on whether it suppresses experimentation — and on cost, A wins on every
line (659-line sim against C's 870 and B's 1,306; no per-level tuning fields
against B's seven; no exhaustive-search dependency to author a room at all against
C, which cannot be authored without one). On suppression, A is the only pillar that
does not charge the player for curiosity, which matters more than anything else on
the table given that Evan's stated goal is *discovery*. A alone is not shippable —
it has one great room, a replayability score of 1, and a memo estimating 10-14
rooms before it becomes combinatorics. Ability-gating is the fix, and a4 is
existence proof that the beat lands; but ability-gating is inert without something
to re-enter a room *for*, which is why the treasure layer comes with it and why the
resource pools stay behind. That combination gets A's clean forcing record, C's
replayability, C's reason-to-return, and the content multiplier, while paying for
none of C's authoring cost and none of B's simulation. The fire tick is a separate,
small, additive bet on the one seeded aha that no static room can ever host.

**The two strongest arguments against my own pick:**

1. **I am discarding the best-measured prototype on a technicality I inferred.**
   Pillar C scores 3.33 forced interactions per level against A's 2.00 and 5/6 GOOD
   against B's 3/6. My entire case for discounting it rests on the denominator
   being "reaches par" rather than "solvable" — but if Evan's pars are honestly set
   (and c3 forcing 5/5 and c6 forcing 6/6 suggest at least some are), then C is
   simply the better pillar and I have thrown out the strongest numbers in the repo
   because I did not like how they were computed. C is also the only pillar with a
   measured answer to replayability, and it earned that number by exhaustive search
   rather than by assertion. My pick's replayability story is an inference about a
   treasure layer that has never been playtested outside the economy that gave it
   meaning.

2. **My pick's central claim is the one claim in this document that nothing
   measures.** Every number in `AUDIT.md` grades a single room in isolation. There
   is no metric anywhere for "does re-entering a1 with the whip create a new
   puzzle," and my own Section 5 analysis concludes it does *not* without a treasure
   layer that does not yet exist. So I am recommending that Evan build two
   unvalidated systems on top of the one pillar whose own author says the design
   drops off after level 4 — and A's proven content, stripped of everything
   aspirational, is exactly one room. If ability-gating turns out to produce
   shortcuts rather than puzzles, Hybrid 2 collapses to Pillar A, and Pillar A is a
   demo.

## 7. Open questions for Evan

Five forks whose answers would change the recommendation.
